The Mountain Bike Mechanic's Guide

Hey there! My name is Connor. Ever since I was a kid, I have loved riding bikes. I loved the freedom it offered and the way it made me feel! So, when I got older and found out that my high school had a mountain bike team, I was all in. I ended up racing competitively all through high school both for Olympus High School and Maybird Cycling, and loved every second of it.

When the Coronavirus Pandemic rocked the world in early 2020, not only did I have more time on my hands to be on my bike, but I also got to spend countless hours in my garage, learning how to tinker and keep my bike in top shape. I had to teach myself, and eventually got a job at a local bike shop when I was trained professionally. As I raced that next year, I quickly came to realize that my experience was abnormal. Most young riders were on bike that were not being kept up adequately, and their experience was being hindered because of it. Throughout this whole experience I realized that there was a large information gap for young riders with limited resources and no experience - they needed a resource they could go to to keep their bikes running smoothly without the expensive price tags.

The purpose of this guide is to provide a resource for young mountain bike riders to learn the basics of bike maintenance and repair without the need for expensive professional services. I hope it helps!

Ride Strong, Be Kind!

-Connor

Basic Bike Repair Checklist

  1. Keeping your Bike Clean
    • Of everything you can do to keep your bike running smoothly, steps 1 and 2 are by far the most crucial. They will solve about 80% of the difficulties entry levell riders will face. Be diligent with these two and eveything else becomes much easier.
    • Materials needed: household bucket(1-3gals.), mild detergent(car soap preferred, dish soap works great as well), clean toilet bowl brush/dish scrubber, old rags, water source(hose preferred)
    • Instructions: Lightly spray bike with a hose, avioding high pressure(can infiltrate and damage bearings). Administer detergent to your brush and scrub the drivetrain, focusing on the cassette and chain. Rinse drivetrain. Use the rag/soap/bucket to wipe down the rest of the bike. Make sure to clean all dirt and grime. Rinse all soap off to finish.
  2. Keeping your Bike Lubricated
    • Cleaning the bike thoroughly without replacing lubricant directly after can be almost as bad as never cleaning you bike at all. Lubricant keeps you bike running smoothly and prevents expedited wear and tear, especially on the chain. Let the bike dry and then follow these steps.
    • Materials needed: chain lubricant, suspension lubricant, rag, paper towels
    • Instructions: Dry chain completely by moving the cranks backwards and holding the chain with the rag. Try to remove any black gunk found on the chain. Once the chain is dry, apply chain lube in a stream while moving the cranks backwards. Wipe off excess. Put a small amount of suspension lubricant on the smooth, shiny part of the stanchions of your fork. Push down and cycle the suspension through multiple times. Wipe off excess.
  3. Checking Air Levels
    • Air levels in your bike can change quickly compared to other variables. Checking them regularly will help ensure a comfortable and safe ride.
    • Materials needed: air pump, tire pressure gauge, shock pump
    • Instructions: Remove cap on each tire and attach pump. Pump to desired pressure, recommended pressure can be found on the side of the tire and varies by manufacturer. Replace valve cap on each tire. For suspension, remove valve cap, attach pump, and pump to recommended pressure. Recommended pressure is typically found on the shock itself or on the manufacturer's website.
  4. Adjusting you Shifting
    • Keeping your shifting properly adjusted will ensure smooth and reliable gear changes and keep your drivetrain runnignfor a long time. It is extremely important because of how expensive it is to replace many parts on the drivetrain. The best way to learn this skill is by watching, so I will include the needed tools and then attach a video from Park Tool, the leading bike tool manufacturer for a step by step walkthrough.
    • Materials needed: set of metric hex wrenches
  5. Adjusting your Brakes
    • Brakes are one of the most important parts of your bike in terms of safety. In order to leave no room for error I have attached a video from a lisenced professional. Enjoy!

This list is in no way comprehensive, but will give you a head start on keeping your bike in good condition and out on the trails. I still recommend getting a comprehensive service done by a professional mechanic at least once a year, preferrably early in the season or before races begin.
